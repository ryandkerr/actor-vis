><span itemprop="author">DICK STEEL</span></a>
                        <meta itemprop="datePublished" content="2006-04-15" />
                              (Singapore)
                        &ndash; <a href="/user/ur0317399/comments?ref_=tt_urv"
>See all my reviews</a>
                    </div>
                    <div>
                        <p itemprop="reviewBody">I was quite surprised that Massive Attack&#39;s Angel was chosen to accompany the opening credits, which consisted for mostly CCTV / voyeuristic shots of a team conducting surveillance on the movements of Harrison Ford&#39;s Jack Stanfield and his family. I know it&#39;s a cool track, but Angel is perhaps becoming one of the more overused tracks in Hollywood pictures already.<br /><br />But it hints well at what Firewall is going to become. Clich&eacute; and overused sequences which will probably make you scream &quot;haven&#39;t I seen this somewhere before&quot;? Firewall offered nothing very new in terms of plot outline, as it contains modified scenes from even Ford&#39;s own works like Air Force One (the family&#39;s survival being threatened, and it&#39;s up to one man to save the day) and The Fugitive (the frame up and one man&#39;s run from the law). Ford has already become comfortable in the role of an all-American one man hero, that this role offered no surprises at all.<br /><br />It&#39;s basically standard fare with the usual chases and action fight sequences. Stansfield is a VP of (network) security of a bank, and has designed the bank&#39;s software, with other operational duties like making sure the bank can respond to external online threats. However, as the saying goes, almost 80% of intrusion are committed knowingly or unknowingly from the inside, and with a head honcho part of the act, you can be sure that he has the know-how to siphon out cash if he wants to.<br /><br />In comes Bill Cox (Paul Bettany), scheming con man, who with his team of merry men, take Stanfield&#39;s family hostage and lapses into the usual psycho-mumbo-jumbo routine. With the family&#39;s antics at escaping, you really wonder if Cox, as a villain, has what it takes to call the shots in a hostage situation. While he might have the upper hand, it&#39;s his weakness in being ruthless to the hostages that proved his downfall, and make him a very weak cinematic villain. Here&#39;s someone who would rather shoot at his own men than to teach them hostages a lesson. Probably he has this noble subscription to &quot;women and children first&quot;. And it doesn&#39;t help when there&#39;s the usual good guy amongst his merry men who offers the family sympathy points.<br /><br />Word of caution though, for those who might think that this film offers some cool technology on screen. Leave the techno-babble at the door, there&#39;s no &quot;firewall&quot; to hack into in the first place, and the &quot;hacking&quot; is actually a no-brainer. And unless the USA offers wireless internet access like everywhere, it&#39;s pretty incredible with what a laptop can do in the movie.<br /><br />It has your expected twists and seemingly smart moments, but nothing that will truly make you go &quot;wow&quot;. The fist fight choreography can get pretty intense, however, there&#39;s no need to reach the end of the road before you know who&#39;s gonna emerge tops. It isn&#39;t exactly a bad movie, but one which doesn&#39;t even have a single sequence that can redeem this picture from really bad mediocrity.</p>
                    </div>
