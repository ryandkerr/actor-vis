     <div id="goofs"  class="txt-block">
        <h4>Goofs</h4>
The rifles everyone carries are Springfield Trapdoor models, which weren't made until 1879, 14 years after the Civil War ended.<br/><br/>Actually the Trapdoor Springfield was introduced in 1865 as a conversion for old muzzle loaders. The .45 caliber Springfield was introduced in 1873.        <a href="trivia?tab=gf&ref_=tt_trv_gf"

                    <strong itemprop="name">Not Especially true to history.....but enjoyable</strong>
                    <span itemprop="reviewRating" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Rating">
                        <meta itemprop="worstRating" content = "1" />

><span itemprop="author">johnboy1</span></a>

                    </div>
                    <div>
                        <p itemprop="reviewBody">This is one of those films that works because of the cast. It's fun to watch actors not well-suited to western films give it their best shot, and do well, despite that fact.<br /><br />Old timers like Noah Beery, Jr and John Doucette were used to the genre, and add to the film greatly.<br /><br />Newcomers (at the time), like James Caan, Michael Sarrazin, Jan-Michael Vincent, Harrison Ford, and Robert Pine would all go on to better things, but they do well here, too. Added to the mix is a TV leftover, Paul Peterson, who's part is small, but well-done.<br /><br />It was obvious that the film was cast and made like it was because of the growing youth market (Wild In The Streets, Psych-Out, Savage Seven, and Chubasco, among others).<br /><br />I love this film very much, and wait patiently for a widescreen DVD to be released. I can only hope I live long enough to see it happen.</p>
